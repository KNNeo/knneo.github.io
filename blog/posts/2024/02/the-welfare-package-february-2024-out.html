Well, let's take the chance to talk about what a comic magazine in Japan is. I have seen them from where I live but of course they have been translated into the native languages here specifically in Chinese (so imported from China of course, or Hong Kong sometimes) and the pages are a lot smaller and compact that I recalled. I remember that a weekly edition of translated Shounen Jump will be out like, every Wednesday or Thursday and my middle (secondary) school classmate will buy one of these to read during their lunch break, they are a huge fan of these stuff. I would see like, the latest chapter of "BLEACH" on them (after seeing them online lol), but a lot smaller than the content on here because they printed 4 pages on A4-sized paper. This though, spanning a colossal 378 pages not including cover pages because those are advertisements for drama (which maybe I take a look later hehe), which on average of 20 pages per chapter, will bring us to around 19 (Edit: 18) different serializations on this thick monthly release!

As to why it is this cheap, I think this is how these things work: It's kind of like a subscription service, where even decades ago, comic publishers have already thought of how to garner a larger audience to like their collective published works and to catch them every month/week on the price it takes to buy a fraction of a full volume when it's out if people liked it. So, to catch up on the latest stuff and before the Internet became a distribution platform, this is the way to go.

It's like watching the latest Anime on TV every week; Those who can't wait will read the latest chapters of their favourite comics and/or be recommended new ones of the same genre as they appear.

As the header of the book has also defined, this book focuses on romantic, lovey-dovey and cute slice-of-life stories (basically all of my favourite Anime genres), and as you will see soon when I open it up, even just the cover will give you a good idea of what you will be getting, and that's how you get your initial audience in, which is what I am doing now.

Imagine this: You walk into a bookstore in Japan, in the streets of Akihabara for example, and you stumble upon their comics section and see stacks of magazines on the shelves. You pick them up and see the cover, maybe know an author or two to give them chance, and buy them at the price you would do to buy a cup of Starbucks. If you read them and like them, then you will come back for that series next month.

This is how, even outside Anime, people live their otaku lives on a regular basis, often alternating through multiple books to find their next favourite, long before it gets mass recognition. As you can see, I'm basically Japanese right now. *dabs* Haha
